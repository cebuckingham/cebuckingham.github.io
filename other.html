Projects

CIOP: an EU-funded individual fellowship aimed at better understanding flow-topography interactions.

DYNOPO: a UK/US NERC/NSF-funded project aimed at understanding processes determining dense water transport flowing out of the Weddell Sea. One mechanism thought to play an important role is mixing in the Orkney Passage.

OSMOSIS: a UK/US NERC/NSF-funded project aimed at understanding processes controlling mixing and re-stratification within the ocean surface boundary layer.

Group for high resolution sea surface temperature (GHRRST): consortium of ocean scientists seeking to develop consistent SST products at high (1-km) resolution for the community.

SEASTAR (stay tuned for additional information): a proposal for an oceanographic satellite aimed at measuring global surface currents at ~1-km horizontal resolution. Led by Christine Gommenginger, Adrien Martin and Betrand Chapron.

OSMOSIS depth-integrated and near-surface currents overlaid on satellite-derived sea surface height (SSH). See Buckingham et al. (2019) for additional information.


Mesoscale (40-200 km) sea surface temperature (SST) and sea surface height (SSH) (low-res) in the South Pacific. SST measurements are from the Advanced Microwave Scanning Radiometer for the Earth Observing System (AMSR-E) and were generated by Remote Sensing Systems (RSS). SSH data are optimally-interpolated sea level anomaly developed and distributed by AVISO/CNES. Both SST and SSH have been spatially high pass-filtered so as to highlight mesoscale features. (See here for additional information on the filter response.)


SST front probability (i.e. the likelihood of observing a front) for the global ocean as determined from microwave SST. These images were obtained by applying an ocean front detection algorithm (Cayula and Cornillon 1992) to swath measurements from AMSR-E SST and is estimated as the number of detections divided by the number of SST measurements over a 3-week period. While the averaging period is 3 weeks, the animation step is one week so as to make the field evolve gradually (i.e., 67% overlap). See Buckingham et al. (2014) for information on what is thought to give rise to these fronts.
