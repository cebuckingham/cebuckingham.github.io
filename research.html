Ocean remote sensing

Although described here as a research topic, this is really an area under development. So much of observational oceanography is "traditional". That is, one goes out on ships and measures temperature, salinity and pressure in the ocean. This is essential but a lot of information can be gleaned from remote sensing imagery. Given my engineering background, I am trying hard to supplement ship-based measurements of submesoscale phenomena with aerial measurements of the same. These phenomena have a tremendous impact on dispersion of surfactants and an aerial observing system (AOS) such as this would be useful for emergency response situations. This work stemmed from a research project in which infrared imagery was used in conjunction with in situ measurements to diagnose observations of a submesoscale frontal instability. Had a clear-sky image not been available from satellite, we would not have observed the front in this manner.

Submesoscale processes and turbulence

An area of research that continues to be investigated is what controls turbulence in the ocean surface boundary layer. Recent research has suggested that submesoscale processes may generate turbulence in the upper oceans. Our research has shown that (at least at 50-m depth in the eastern North Atlantic) that surface-forced turbulence (i.e. due to winds, waves, buoyancy fluxes) are an order of magnitude larger than submesoscale-forced turbulence. This being said, we also found that the energy dissipated by symmetric instability is non-negligible, perhaps approaching values of 10% of the wind-work into the oceans. The conclusion is that surface processes dominate vertical turbulent energy budgets but that submesoscale processes are important for horizontal energy budgets, having a potentially profound effect upon large-scale ocean circulation. This work is summarized here.

I am presently a Marie Sklodowska-Curie Individual Fellow at the University of Bretagne Occidentale near Brest, France. I am learning how to model oceanic flows in realistic configurations. Together with Jonathan Gula, we are seeking to understand what controls turbulence within a deep-sea canyon in the southern Weddell Sea. To better constrain our simulations, we are using moored instruments and ship-collected measurements that my colleagues and I (while I was at the British Antarctic Survey) collected in April 2017.

Geostrophic turbulence: oceanic mesoscale eddies & jets

My dissertation research centred around the use of satellite measurements to investigate whether or not jets exist in the oceans. Often referred to as striations or bands, these jet-like features are only observed in time averaged fields, such as sea surface height (SSH) and sea surface temperature (SST) shown to the left. They are ubiquitous in the global ocean, are associated with eastward- and westward-flowing currents, and have magnitudes comparable or smaller than the mesoscale eddy field. Our research has focused on the detection and discrimination of jets from the surrounding mesoscale eddies, where we find much of this energy is attributed to the mesoscale eddy field. This work is summarized in two papers, one pertaining to SSH and the other SST.

It is possible that jets similar to those on Jupiter exist in the global oceans. Their existence is predicted from theory but they remain elusive in the oceans. One reason may be energy loss due to baroclinic instabilities that occurs on the periphery of mesoscale eddies. Another is symmetric instability initiated by sustained winds and buoyancy fluxes at ocean fronts. Finally, increased drag as a result of bottom topography might also explain this.
